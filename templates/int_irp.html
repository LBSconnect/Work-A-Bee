Work-A-Beez Incident Response Plan (IRP)

Status: Complete · Classification: Internal Confidential · Last updated: {{ updated.strftime('%B %d, %Y') }}

Version: 1.0
Effective Date: July 23, 2026
Classification: Internal Confidential
Owner: Linton Business Solutions, LLC (LBS)

1. Purpose

The purpose of this Incident Response Plan ("IRP") is to establish a structured and repeatable process for identifying, reporting, investigating, containing, eradicating, recovering from, and documenting information security incidents affecting the Work-A-Beez platform. The objectives of this plan are to:

  • Protect Customer Data.
  • Minimize business disruption.
  • Preserve system integrity.
  • Reduce recovery time.
  • Meet contractual and legal obligations.
  • Coordinate internal and external communications.
  • Improve future security posture through lessons learned.

This Incident Response Plan supports the Work-A-Beez:

  • Security Policy
  • Business Continuity Plan (BCP)
  • Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)
  • Data Processing Addendum (DPA)
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA)

2. Scope

This plan applies to all security incidents affecting:

  • Production systems
  • Cloud infrastructure
  • Customer Data
  • Employee accounts
  • Administrative accounts
  • APIs
  • Authentication systems
  • Source code repositories
  • CI/CD pipelines
  • Third-party services supporting Work-A-Beez

This plan applies to all employees, contractors, and approved third-party service providers supporting Work-A-Beez.

3. Incident Response Objectives

The Incident Response Program seeks to:

  • Detect incidents quickly.
  • Limit operational impact.
  • Contain threats.
  • Preserve evidence.
  • Restore normal operations.
  • Protect customer trust.
  • Meet applicable reporting obligations.
  • Continuously improve organizational security.

4. Guiding Principles

All incident response activities should follow these principles:

  • Safety of personnel
  • Protection of Customer Data
  • Preservation of evidence
  • Timely communication
  • Least privilege
  • Need-to-know information sharing
  • Accurate documentation
  • Continuous improvement

When priorities conflict, protecting Customer Data and preserving evidence should take precedence over restoring affected systems.

LBS maintains an Incident Response Team responsible for coordinating cybersecurity incidents. Depending on company size, one individual may fulfill multiple roles. Executive Sponsor Responsibilities:

  • Executive oversight
  • Major business decisions
  • Customer communication approval
  • Regulatory decision support

Incident Commander Responsible for:

  • Overall incident coordination

  • Resource allocation
  • Incident timeline management
  • Executive reporting

Security Lead Responsible for:

  • Threat analysis
  • Evidence preservation
  • Security investigation
  • Containment strategy
  • Security validation

Engineering Lead Responsible for:

  • Technical remediation
  • Infrastructure recovery
  • Application restoration
  • Root cause remediation

Customer Success Lead Responsible for:

  • Customer communications
  • Support coordination
  • Customer impact tracking

Legal and Compliance Advisor Where appropriate, legal counsel may assist with:

  • Regulatory obligations
  • Contractual requirements
  • Privacy considerations
  • Law enforcement coordination
  • Litigation preservation

6. Incident Severity Levels

Incidents are classified according to business impact. Severity 1 – Critical Examples:

  • Confirmed Customer Data breach
  • Active ransomware
  • Complete production compromise
  • Large-scale credential compromise
  • Widespread service outage caused by malicious activity

Business Impact: Critical business interruption requiring immediate executive involvement. Severity 2 – High Examples:

  • Privileged account compromise
  • Significant malware infection
  • Limited Customer Data exposure
  • API compromise
  • Authentication failure

Business Impact: Major operational disruption requiring immediate technical response. Severity 3 – Medium Examples:

  • Phishing affecting a limited number of users
  • Malware on a non-critical workstation
  • Failed security controls
  • Isolated unauthorized access attempts

Business Impact: Limited operational impact with manageable risk. Severity 4 – Low Examples:

  • Suspicious email reports
  • Security policy violations
  • Failed login investigations
  • Minor configuration weaknesses

Business Impact: Minimal operational disruption.

7. Incident Categories

Security incidents may include:

  • Malware
  • Ransomware
  • Phishing
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC)
  • Credential theft
  • Insider threat
  • Unauthorized access
  • Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS)
  • Application vulnerability exploitation
  • Cloud infrastructure compromise
  • Data leakage
  • Lost or stolen administrative devices
  • Third-party vendor compromise

Additional categories may be defined as the threat landscape evolves.

8. Incident Reporting

Personnel should report suspected security incidents immediately through approved internal reporting channels. Reports should include, when available:

  • Date and time observed
  • System affected
  • Description of activity
  • Screenshots or logs
  • User accounts involved
  • Business impact
  • Reporter contact information

Prompt reporting improves containment and recovery efforts.

9. Detection Sources

Security incidents may be identified through:

  • Security monitoring tools
  • Application logs
  • Infrastructure monitoring
  • Customer reports
  • Employee observations
  • Third-party vendors
  • Threat intelligence notifications
  • Automated alerts
  • Vulnerability scanners
  • Cloud provider notifications

All credible reports should be evaluated promptly.

10. Initial Incident Assessment

Upon receiving a report, the Incident Response Team should:

  1. Confirm the event.
  2. Determine whether it is a security incident.
  3. Assign an initial severity level.
  4. Identify affected systems.
  5. Estimate customer impact.
  6. Determine whether escalation is required.
  7. Initiate documentation.
  8. Begin containment planning.

Severity may be adjusted as additional information becomes available.

11. Incident Documentation

Every significant incident should have an incident record documenting:

  • Incident identifier
  • Date and time detected
  • Reporter
  • Incident category
  • Severity level
  • Systems affected
  • Timeline of actions
  • Individuals involved
  • Customer impact
  • Root cause
  • Resolution
  • Lessons learned

Accurate documentation supports audits, regulatory compliance, and continuous improvement.

Part 2 – Incident Handling Procedures and Response Runbooks

Version: 1.0

12. Evidence Preservation

When a security incident is suspected, preserving evidence is essential to support investigation, recovery, legal obligations, and potential regulatory inquiries. Personnel should:

  • Avoid unnecessary changes to affected systems.
  • Preserve relevant system logs.
  • Capture timestamps.
  • Record observed activity.
  • Document actions performed during the response.
  • Preserve volatile data when appropriate.
  • Secure copies of relevant configuration information.

Evidence should be collected only by authorized personnel whenever practical.

13. Chain of Custody

When evidence is collected, LBS should maintain documentation describing:

  • Evidence identifier
  • Description of evidence
  • Date and time collected
  • Collector's name
  • Storage location
  • Individuals who accessed the evidence
  • Reason for each transfer
  • Final disposition

Maintaining a documented chain of custody supports the integrity and admissibility of evidence where required.

14. Containment Strategy

Containment activities aim to prevent further damage while preserving critical evidence. Containment actions may include:

  • Isolating affected systems
  • Disabling compromised accounts
  • Blocking malicious IP addresses
  • Revoking API credentials
  • Restricting administrative access
  • Disabling affected integrations
  • Segregating compromised workloads
  • Temporarily suspending impacted services

Containment actions should balance operational continuity with security requirements.

15. Malware Response Runbook

When malware is suspected:

  1. Isolate the affected system.
  2. Preserve evidence.
  3. Determine malware scope.
  4. Identify affected users.
  5. Review system logs.
  6. Scan adjacent systems.
  7. Remove malicious software using approved procedures.
  8. Apply security updates.
  9. Restore from trusted backups if necessary.
  10. Validate system integrity before returning the system to service.

Recovered systems should be monitored closely for recurring indicators of compromise.

16. Ransomware Response Runbook

If ransomware is suspected or confirmed:

  1. Immediately isolate affected systems.
  2. Disable network connectivity where appropriate.
  3. Preserve forensic evidence.
  4. Notify executive leadership.
  5. Activate the Incident Response Team.
  6. Assess the scope of encryption.
  7. Determine whether Customer Data has been exfiltrated.
  8. Restore systems from verified backups when appropriate.
  9. Rotate potentially compromised credentials.
  10. Validate restored systems before production use.

LBS does not maintain a policy of paying ransom demands. Any exception would require executive approval, legal review, and consideration of applicable laws and sanctions.

17. Phishing Response Runbook

When phishing activity is reported:

  • Collect the original email.
  • Analyze headers and message content.
  • Determine whether credentials were entered.
  • Disable compromised accounts if necessary.
  • Reset affected credentials.
  • Review authentication logs.
  • Block malicious domains or senders where feasible.
  • Notify potentially affected personnel.
  • Conduct user awareness follow-up as appropriate.

Patterns of phishing activity should be reviewed to improve preventative controls.

18. Credential Compromise Response

If account credentials are suspected to be compromised:

  • Disable or lock the affected account.
  • Require a password reset.
  • Revoke active sessions.
  • Review recent authentication activity.
  • Verify MFA enrollment and operation.
  • Rotate API keys or service credentials if affected.
  • Confirm that administrative privileges remain appropriate.

Privileged account compromises require immediate investigation and escalation.

19. Insider Threat Response

Potential insider threats should be handled discreetly and in coordination with appropriate leadership. Response activities may include:

  • Reviewing access logs.
  • Preserving evidence.
  • Restricting access where warranted.
  • Coordinating with Human Resources and legal counsel, if applicable.
  • Monitoring for additional unauthorized activity.
  • Assessing potential Customer Data exposure.

Investigations should respect applicable employment laws and privacy obligations.

20. Cloud Infrastructure Compromise

If cloud infrastructure is suspected to be compromised:

  • Assess the scope of the compromise.
  • Restrict administrative access.
  • Review cloud audit logs.
  • Rotate cloud credentials and access keys as appropriate.
  • Validate infrastructure configurations.
  • Restore affected workloads from trusted sources.
  • Coordinate with the cloud provider if required.

Infrastructure changes should be documented and verified before restoring production traffic.

21. Third-Party Security Incidents

If a third-party service provider experiences a security incident that may affect Work-A-Beez:

  • Evaluate the provider's notification.
  • Assess potential impact on Work-A-Beez.
  • Determine whether Customer Data may be affected.
  • Coordinate remediation with the provider.
  • Implement temporary workarounds if feasible.
  • Notify affected Customers when required by law or contract.

Vendor-related incidents should be tracked until resolution.

22. Data Exposure Assessment

For incidents involving potential unauthorized access to information, the Incident Response Team should assess:

  • Categories of information involved.
  • Number of affected customers or users.
  • Duration of exposure.
  • Likelihood of unauthorized access or exfiltration.
  • Whether encryption or other protections were in place.
  • Potential legal, contractual, or regulatory obligations.

This assessment informs notification and remediation decisions.

23. Regulatory Notification Considerations

Following confirmation of a qualifying incident, LBS will evaluate whether notification obligations exist under:

  • Applicable privacy laws.
  • Customer contracts.
  • Regulatory requirements.
  • Industry-specific obligations.

Notification timing and content will be determined in consultation with legal counsel where appropriate.

24. Customer Notification

When customer notification is required, communications should include, where appropriate:

  • A description of the incident.
  • The date or estimated timeframe of occurrence.
  • Categories of information potentially affected.
  • Actions taken by LBS.
  • Recommended customer actions.
  • Available support resources.
  • Contact information for additional assistance.

Notifications should be factual, timely, and avoid speculation.

25. Law Enforcement Coordination

LBS may coordinate with law enforcement or other appropriate authorities when:

  • Criminal activity is suspected.
  • Required by law.
  • Appropriate to support investigation or recovery.

The decision to involve law enforcement will be made by executive leadership in consultation with legal counsel, considering the nature of the incident and applicable legal obligations.

26. Communication Management

During an active incident, communications should be coordinated to ensure consistency and accuracy. Communication audiences may include:

  • Executive leadership
  • Employees
  • Customers
  • Vendors
  • Legal counsel
  • Insurance providers
  • Regulatory authorities
  • Law enforcement, where appropriate

Only authorized spokespersons may make public statements regarding security incidents. All communications should be documented as part of the incident record.

Part 3 – Recovery, Governance, Testing, and Continuous Improvement

Version: 1.0

27. Recovery Procedures

Following successful containment and eradication of a security incident, Linton Business Solutions, LLC ("LBS") will begin recovery activities to safely restore affected systems and business operations.

Recovery objectives include:

  • Restoring production services.
  • Verifying system integrity.
  • Confirming Customer Data availability.
  • Preventing reoccurrence of the incident.
  • Returning business operations to normal.

Recovery should proceed in a controlled manner using approved change management procedures.

28. Recovery Validation

Prior to restoring production access, the Incident Response Team should validate that affected systems are operating securely.

Validation activities may include:

Infrastructure

  • Compute resources operational
  • Network connectivity restored
  • Security groups validated
  • Firewall rules verified
  • Monitoring agents active

Application

  • Successful user authentication
  • Employee clock-in functionality
  • Employee clock-out functionality
  • Payroll calculations verified
  • Administrative portal operational
  • API connectivity confirmed

Security

  • Logging operational
  • Security alerts functioning
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) verified where applicable
  • Privileged accounts reviewed
  • Temporary containment measures removed or documented

Only after successful validation should systems return to full production use.

29. Root Cause Analysis

Following recovery, the Incident Response Team will perform a Root Cause Analysis (RCA) to identify the underlying factors that contributed to the incident.

The RCA should address:

  • How the incident occurred
  • Initial attack vector
  • Systems affected
  • Security controls that functioned as intended
  • Security controls that failed or were bypassed
  • Timeline of events
  • Contributing operational factors
  • Opportunities to reduce future risk

The goal of the RCA is continuous improvement rather than assigning blame.

30. Post-Incident Review

A formal Post-Incident Review (PIR) should be conducted for all Severity 1 and Severity 2 incidents, and for other incidents as appropriate.

The review should evaluate:

  • Incident timeline
  • Response effectiveness
  • Communication effectiveness
  • Technical recovery
  • Customer impact
  • Regulatory considerations
  • Vendor performance
  • Documentation quality
  • Lessons learned

Findings should be documented and shared with appropriate stakeholders.

31. Corrective Action Management

Recommendations resulting from the Root Cause Analysis or Post-Incident Review should be tracked through a formal corrective action process.

Each corrective action should include:

  • Unique identifier
  • Description
  • Risk level
  • Assigned owner
  • Target completion date
  • Current status
  • Validation of completion

Corrective actions may include:

  • Security control improvements
  • Infrastructure enhancements
  • Software updates
  • Process changes
  • Documentation revisions
  • Training initiatives
  • Vendor management improvements

Management should review corrective action status on a regular basis.

32. Incident Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

To evaluate the effectiveness of the Incident Response Program, LBS may monitor metrics including:

  • Number of incidents by severity
  • Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)
  • Mean Time to Acknowledge (MTTA)
  • Mean Time to Contain (MTTC)
  • Mean Time to Recover (MTTR)
  • Percentage of incidents meeting response objectives
  • Number of repeat incidents
  • Phishing simulation success rates (if conducted)
  • Number of security awareness training completions
  • Number of unresolved corrective actions

Metrics should be reviewed periodically to identify trends and opportunities for improvement.

33. Security Awareness and Training

Personnel with incident response responsibilities should receive periodic training appropriate to their roles.

Training topics may include:

  • Incident identification
  • Reporting procedures
  • Evidence preservation
  • Secure communications
  • Phishing recognition
  • Ransomware awareness
  • Data handling requirements
  • Customer communication expectations

New personnel assigned to the Incident Response Team should receive orientation before participating in active incident response activities.

34. Incident Response Exercises

LBS should periodically test its incident response capabilities through structured exercises.

Exercise types may include:

  • Tabletop exercises
  • Technical simulations
  • Phishing simulations
  • Ransomware response scenarios
  • Cloud compromise scenarios
  • Insider threat scenarios
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC) simulations

Exercises should identify strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities to improve procedures.

35. Documentation and Record Retention

Incident response documentation may include:

  • Incident reports
  • Investigation notes
  • Evidence logs
  • Chain of custody records
  • Communication records
  • Recovery documentation
  • Corrective action plans
  • Lessons learned

Retention periods should align with legal, contractual, regulatory, and operational requirements.

Access to incident documentation should be restricted to authorized personnel.

36. Coordination with Other Plans

This Incident Response Plan is designed to operate in conjunction with the following Work-A-Beez governance documents:

  • Security Policy
  • Business Continuity Plan (BCP)
  • Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA)
  • Data Processing Addendum (DPA)
  • Privacy Policy
  • Acceptable Use Policy

Where a cybersecurity incident escalates into a broader operational disruption, the Business Continuity Plan and Disaster Recovery Plan may be activated.

37. Plan Review and Maintenance

This Incident Response Plan should be reviewed:

  • At least annually
  • Following major security incidents
  • After significant architectural changes
  • Following incident response exercises
  • After changes in applicable legal or contractual obligations
  • When new threat intelligence indicates a material change in risk

Plan revisions should be version-controlled and approved by executive management.

38. Plan Distribution

Controlled copies of this Incident Response Plan should be made available to:

  • Executive leadership
  • Engineering leadership
  • Security personnel
  • Customer Success leadership
  • Operations leadership
  • Legal counsel, where appropriate
  • Authorized auditors under applicable confidentiality obligations

Access to operational response procedures should be restricted to authorized personnel.

Appendix B – Incident Response Checklist

  1. Receive and verify the incident report.
  2. Classify the incident severity.
  3. Activate the Incident Response Team if required.
  4. Preserve evidence.
  5. Contain the threat.
  6. Assess business and customer impact.
  7. Eradicate the root cause.
  8. Recover affected systems.
  9. Validate production readiness.
  10. Notify affected parties when required.
  11. Conduct a post-incident review.
  12. Track corrective actions to completion.
