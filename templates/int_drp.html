Work-A-Beez Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)

Version: 1.0
Effective Date: July 23, 2026
Classification: Internal Confidential
Owner: Linton Business Solutions, LLC (LBS)

1. Purpose

The purpose of this Disaster Recovery Plan ("DRP") is to define the technical procedures and governance required to recover the Work-A-Bee platform following a disaster or catastrophic event affecting production systems.

This plan is intended to:

  • Restore critical production services.
  • Protect Customer Data.
  • Minimize operational downtime.
  • Support business continuity objectives.
  • Provide a structured technical recovery process.
  • Establish recovery responsibilities.
  • Validate restoration before returning to production.

This DRP supports the Work-A-Bee:

  • Business Continuity Plan (BCP)
  • Security Policy
  • Incident Response Plan
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA)

2. Scope

This Disaster Recovery Plan applies to:

  • Production application infrastructure
  • Databases
  • Authentication services
  • APIs
  • Administrative portal
  • Employee portal
  • Reporting services
  • Billing platform
  • Monitoring systems
  • Backup repositories
  • Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) assets
  • Supporting cloud infrastructure

This plan applies only to production systems supporting the Work-A-Bee SaaS platform.

3. Objectives

The primary objectives of disaster recovery are to:

  • Restore production availability.
  • Recover Customer Data.
  • Protect system integrity.
  • Maintain platform security.
  • Minimize Recovery Time Objective (RTO).
  • Minimize Recovery Point Objective (RPO).
  • Restore critical customer functionality.
  • Verify successful recovery before returning to normal operations.

4. Disaster Recovery Principles

The Work-A-Bee disaster recovery program is based on the following principles:

  • Security First
  • Customer Data Protection
  • Least Downtime
  • Documented Recovery Procedures
  • Recovery Validation
  • Continuous Improvement
  • Automation where practical
  • Defense in Depth

Recovery decisions should prioritize data integrity over recovery speed whenever those priorities conflict.

5. Disaster Recovery Governance

Overall responsibility for disaster recovery rests with executive leadership and designated technical personnel.

Executive Management

Responsible for:

  • Disaster declaration
  • Resource authorization
  • Customer communications

Disaster Recovery Coordinator

  • Coordinating recovery activities

  • Tracking recovery status
  • Reporting progress

Engineering Team

Responsible for:

  • Infrastructure restoration
  • Application deployment
  • Database recovery
  • Validation testing

Security Team

Responsible for:

  • Security validation
  • Credential rotation where required
  • Threat assessment
  • Recovery authorization from a security perspective

Customer Success

Responsible for:

  • Customer communications
  • Support coordination
  • Service updates

7. Disaster Scenarios

Examples of events that may require activation of this DRP include:

Infrastructure Failure

  • Complete cloud region outage
  • Storage subsystem failure
  • Network failure
  • Virtual infrastructure failure

Cybersecurity Event

  • Ransomware
  • Unauthorized access
  • Destructive malware
  • Privilege compromise
  • Widespread credential theft

Data Loss

  • Database corruption
  • Accidental deletion
  • Storage failure
  • Backup corruption

Application Failure

  • Critical deployment failure
  • Software defect affecting production
  • Configuration corruption
  • Failed upgrade

Third-Party Failure

  • Identity provider outage
  • Cloud provider disruption
  • DNS provider outage
  • Payment processor outage
  • Email delivery failure

8. Disaster Declaration Criteria

A disaster may be declared when one or more of the following conditions exists:

  • Extended production outage.
  • Loss of critical customer functionality.
  • Widespread infrastructure failure.
  • Significant data loss.
  • Confirmed catastrophic cybersecurity incident.
  • Recovery cannot reasonably be completed through standard operational procedures.

Only authorized executive leadership may declare a disaster.

9. Recovery Priorities

Recovery activities generally follow this order:

1. Personnel safety

2. Incident containment

3. Protection of Customer Data

4. Authentication services

5. Core application infrastructure

6. Timekeeping services

7. Payroll processing

8. Administrative portal

9. Reporting

10. Ancillary services

Recovery priorities may be adjusted based on the nature of the disaster and customer impact.

10. Recovery Strategy

The disaster recovery strategy is designed around the following objectives:

  • Restore infrastructure from approved configurations.
  • Recover databases from verified backups.
  • Deploy the latest validated application release.
  • Verify system integrity.
  • Validate customer authentication.
  • Restore customer functionality.
  • Resume production operations.

Whenever practical, automated deployment and Infrastructure-as-Code processes should be used to reduce recovery time and configuration drift.

11. Recovery Dependencies

Successful recovery depends on the availability of critical dependencies, including:

Cloud Infrastructure

  • Storage

Third-Party Services

Internal Assets

  • Documentation

Dependencies should be reviewed periodically as the platform architecture evolves.

Part 2 – Technical Recovery Procedures and Runbooks

Version: 1.0

12. Backup Strategy

Linton Business Solutions, LLC ("LBS") maintains backup procedures designed to support recovery from catastrophic failures while minimizing potential data loss.

The backup strategy is based on the following principles:

  • Automated backup creation
  • Encryption of backup data
  • Multiple recovery points
  • Periodic restoration testing
  • Restricted administrative access
  • Secure storage
  • Documented recovery procedures

Backups are intended to support disaster recovery and are not a substitute for customer-maintained business records.

13. Backup Schedule

The following represents the standard backup objectives for production systems.

System Backup Frequency Retention Target
Production Database Hourly incremental 30 days
Daily Full Database Backup Daily 90 days
File Storage Daily 90 days
Configuration Data Daily 90 days
Infrastructure Configuration After approved changes Current + previous versions
Application Source Code Continuous version control Permanent while repository is active
Audit Logs Daily archival 365 days
Monitoring Configuration Weekly 180 days

Retention periods may be adjusted to satisfy legal, contractual, or operational requirements.

14. Backup Security

Backups should be protected using commercially reasonable security controls.

Security measures include:

  • Encryption at rest
  • Encryption during transmission
  • Role-based access controls
  • Multi-factor authentication for administrative access
  • Audit logging of backup operations
  • Periodic verification of backup integrity

Only authorized personnel may access backup repositories.

15. Infrastructure Recovery

If production infrastructure becomes unavailable, engineering personnel should perform recovery using documented Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) templates where available.

Recovery activities generally include:

  1. Provision networking.
  2. Deploy compute resources.
  3. Configure storage.
  4. Restore infrastructure configuration.
  5. Deploy monitoring agents.
  6. Verify security controls.
  7. Confirm connectivity.

Infrastructure deployments should use approved configurations to minimize configuration drift.

16. Database Recovery

Database restoration should follow a controlled process to preserve data integrity.

Typical steps include:

  1. Verify backup availability.
  2. Confirm backup integrity.
  3. Provision replacement database resources.
  4. Restore the selected recovery point.
  5. Validate schema integrity.
  6. Verify application connectivity.
  7. Confirm successful authentication.
  8. Validate critical business data.
  9. Resume production access after approval.

Database recovery should be documented for each disaster event.

17. Application Recovery

Following infrastructure restoration, application services should be redeployed using the latest approved production release.

Recovery procedures generally include:

  • Deploy production application
  • Restore configuration settings
  • Configure environment variables
  • Restore secrets from approved secret management systems
  • Verify service dependencies
  • Execute smoke tests
  • Validate API connectivity
  • Verify user authentication
  • Enable monitoring

Only approved production releases should be deployed during disaster recovery.

18. Identity and Authentication Recovery

Authentication services are considered Tier 1 recovery priorities.

Recovery procedures may include:

  • Restore identity provider connectivity
  • Validate authentication endpoints
  • Restore administrator access
  • Verify Multi-Factor Authentication functionality
  • Confirm role-based access permissions
  • Validate session management
  • Review privileged accounts

Administrative credentials should be reviewed after significant security incidents.

19. Secret Management Recovery

Sensitive configuration values should never be manually recreated without authorization.

Recovery procedures include restoring approved secrets, such as:

  • API keys
  • Database credentials
  • Encryption keys
  • Service account credentials
  • OAuth client secrets
  • SMTP credentials
  • Cloud provider credentials

All restored secrets should be verified prior to production activation.

20. Encryption Key Recovery

Encryption keys are critical security assets.

Recovery procedures should ensure:

  • Secure restoration
  • Access limited to authorized personnel
  • Key integrity verification
  • Rotation when compromise is suspected
  • Documentation of recovery activities

Compromised keys should be replaced before production services resume where reasonably practicable.

21. DNS Recovery

If DNS services are affected, recovery should prioritize:

  • Administrative portals
  • Customer-facing application
  • API endpoints
  • Status page
  • Support portal

DNS changes should be validated using multiple geographic locations before customer notification.

22. Network Recovery

Network restoration activities may include:

  • Firewall configuration
  • Routing validation
  • VPN connectivity
  • Security group verification
  • Load balancer restoration
  • SSL certificate validation
  • External connectivity testing

Production access should remain restricted until security validation is complete.

23. Monitoring Recovery

Operational monitoring should be restored before declaring recovery complete.

Monitoring restoration includes:

  • Infrastructure monitoring
  • Application monitoring
  • Database monitoring
  • Log aggregation
  • Alerting systems
  • Health checks
  • Performance dashboards

Monitoring confirms that recovered systems continue operating normally after restoration.

24. Recovery Validation

Technical validation should be completed before returning systems to production.

Validation includes:

Infrastructure

  • Compute resources operational
  • Storage healthy
  • Networking functional
  • Security groups verified

Database

  • Recovery point confirmed
  • Data integrity validated
  • Replication functioning where applicable

Application

  • Login successful
  • Employee clock-in operational
  • Employee clock-out operational
  • Time entries saved
  • Payroll calculations validated
  • Reports generated successfully

Security

  • Authentication verified
  • MFA operational where enabled
  • Administrative access reviewed
  • Logging operational
  • Monitoring alerts active

25. Technical Recovery Runbook

The following high-level runbook should guide engineering personnel:

  1. Confirm disaster declaration.
  2. Assemble recovery team.
  3. Assess infrastructure availability.
  4. Restore networking.
  5. Restore compute resources.
  6. Restore storage.
  7. Restore databases.
  8. Deploy application services.
  9. Restore secrets.
  10. Verify authentication.
  11. Execute smoke testing.
  12. Execute regression validation of critical workflows.
  13. Enable monitoring and alerting.
  14. Validate backups.
  15. Obtain recovery approval.
  16. Resume production operations.

Each completed step should be documented with timestamps and responsible personnel.

26. Recovery Documentation

During each disaster recovery event, engineering teams should document:

  • Event timeline
  • Recovery decisions
  • Commands executed (where appropriate)
  • Backup versions restored
  • Configuration changes
  • Validation results
  • Outstanding issues
  • Lessons learned

These records support audits, continuous improvement, and future recovery efforts.

Part 3 – Testing, Governance, Continuous Improvement, and Appendices

Version: 1.0

27. Disaster Recovery Testing Program

Disaster recovery procedures must be validated through planned testing to ensure technical readiness and organizational preparedness.

The objectives of the testing program are to:

  • Verify recovery procedures.
  • Confirm Recovery Time Objective (RTO) capabilities.
  • Confirm Recovery Point Objective (RPO) capabilities.
  • Validate backup integrity.
  • Measure recovery performance.
  • Identify documentation gaps.
  • Improve recovery readiness.

Testing should be documented and reviewed by engineering leadership and executive management.

28. Types of Disaster Recovery Tests

LBS may perform one or more of the following disaster recovery exercises.

Documentation Review

Review recovery documentation for:

  • Accuracy
  • Completeness
  • Technical currency
  • Contact information
  • Recovery procedures

Tabletop Exercise

Engineering and leadership teams simulate disaster scenarios without affecting production systems.

Typical scenarios include:

  • Cloud provider outage
  • Database corruption
  • Ransomware attack
  • Identity provider failure
  • Regional infrastructure outage
  • Large-scale deployment failure

Component Recovery Test

Individual recovery procedures are tested independently.

Examples include:

  • Database restoration
  • Infrastructure deployment
  • Backup restoration
  • DNS recovery
  • Authentication recovery

Full Disaster Recovery Exercise

A complete end-to-end recovery simulation validating:

  • Infrastructure restoration
  • Database recovery
  • Application deployment
  • Authentication
  • Monitoring
  • Customer workflows
  • Recovery validation

Where feasible, these exercises should be conducted in a non-production environment.

29. Failover Procedures

When supported by the platform architecture, failover procedures may include:

  • Redirecting traffic to standby infrastructure
  • Activating alternate compute resources
  • Promoting secondary databases
  • Restoring application services
  • Verifying customer authentication
  • Monitoring service stability

Failover should follow documented procedures and be approved by authorized technical leadership.

30. Failback Procedures

After the primary environment is restored, LBS may transition services back to the primary production environment.

Failback activities include:

  • Synchronizing data
  • Validating data consistency
  • Restoring primary routing
  • Verifying application health
  • Monitoring stability
  • Confirming customer functionality

Failback should be planned to minimize customer disruption.

31. Recovery Performance Metrics

Following each disaster recovery exercise or actual event, LBS should evaluate performance against established objectives.

Metrics may include:

  • Actual Recovery Time (RTO achieved)
  • Actual Recovery Point (RPO achieved)
  • Duration of service interruption
  • Number of systems restored
  • Number of validation failures
  • Customer impact
  • Support ticket volume
  • Time to customer notification
  • Time to executive notification

These metrics support continual improvement of the disaster recovery program.

32. Third-Party Recovery Coordination

Recovery efforts may require coordination with external service providers.

Examples include:

  • Cloud infrastructure providers
  • Identity and authentication providers
  • Payment processors
  • DNS providers
  • Email delivery providers
  • Monitoring vendors
  • Security service providers

Vendor contact procedures and escalation paths should be maintained separately and reviewed periodically.

33. Customer Communications During Recovery

When customer-facing services are affected, LBS will provide timely updates through appropriate communication channels.

Communications may include:

  • Initial incident notification
  • Service impact summary
  • Recovery progress updates
  • Estimated restoration timelines (when reasonably available)
  • Service restoration confirmation
  • Post-incident summary, when appropriate

Updates should be factual, timely, and avoid speculation.

34. Post-Recovery Review

Following every major recovery event, LBS should conduct a structured review to evaluate:

  • Recovery effectiveness
  • Achievement of RTO and RPO targets
  • Root cause
  • Recovery challenges
  • Customer communications
  • Vendor performance
  • Documentation quality
  • Opportunities for improvement

Findings should be documented and retained according to organizational record retention requirements.

35. Corrective Action Program

Issues identified during testing or actual recovery events should be entered into a corrective action process.

Corrective actions may include:

  • Infrastructure improvements
  • Process enhancements
  • Additional automation
  • Documentation revisions
  • Training initiatives
  • Monitoring improvements
  • Vendor changes
  • Security enhancements

Each action should include:

  • Description
  • Owner
  • Priority
  • Target completion date
  • Completion status

Management should periodically review outstanding corrective actions.

36. Continuous Improvement

Disaster recovery planning is an ongoing process.

LBS will periodically evaluate:

  • Technology changes
  • Cloud architecture updates
  • Security threats
  • Customer requirements
  • Vendor capabilities
  • Regulatory developments
  • Industry best practices

The Disaster Recovery Plan will be updated to reflect significant organizational or technical changes.

37. Plan Review and Maintenance

This Disaster Recovery Plan should be reviewed:

  • At least annually
  • Following major infrastructure changes
  • After significant production incidents
  • Following disaster recovery exercises
  • After changes to critical vendors
  • When new regulatory or contractual requirements arise

Plan revisions should be version-controlled and approved by appropriate management.

38. Distribution and Access

Controlled copies of this Disaster Recovery Plan should be made available to personnel with disaster recovery responsibilities, including:

  • Executive leadership
  • Engineering leadership
  • Security leadership
  • Operations
  • Customer Success leadership
  • Authorized auditors, when appropriate

Access to detailed technical recovery procedures should be limited to authorized personnel.
