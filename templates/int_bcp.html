Work-A-Beez Business Continuity Plan (BCP)

Status: Complete · Classification: Internal Confidential · Last updated: {{ updated.strftime('%B %d, %Y') }}

Version: 1.0
Effective Date: July 23, 2026
Owner: Linton Business Solutions, LLC (LBS)

1. Purpose

The purpose of this Business Continuity Plan ("BCP") is to establish the policies, procedures, and responsibilities necessary to maintain or restore critical business operations following an event that disrupts the normal operation of the Work-A-Bee platform.

This plan is intended to:

  • Protect employees, customers, and business operations.
  • Minimize service interruptions.
  • Reduce financial and operational impacts.
  • Preserve Customer Data.
  • Maintain regulatory and contractual obligations.
  • Support timely recovery of critical services.

This BCP complements the Work-A-Bee:

  • Security Policy
  • Incident Response Plan
  • Disaster Recovery Plan
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA)

2. Scope

This Business Continuity Plan applies to:

  • Production cloud infrastructure
  • Customer support operations
  • Engineering operations
  • Product management
  • Administrative services
  • Billing operations
  • Identity and authentication services
  • Customer communications
  • Third-party hosted services supporting Work-A-Bee

This plan applies to all LBS personnel, contractors, and approved third-party providers supporting the Work-A-Bee platform.

3. Objectives

The primary objectives of this Business Continuity Plan are to:

  • Protect life and safety.
  • Maintain essential business operations.
  • Restore critical customer-facing services.
  • Preserve the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of Customer Data.
  • Maintain customer confidence.
  • Coordinate internal response activities.
  • Support compliance with contractual and legal obligations.
  • Continuously improve organizational resilience.

4. Business Continuity Governance

Overall responsibility for the Business Continuity Program rests with executive management.

Key responsibilities include:

Executive Management

  • Approves continuity strategies
  • Allocates recovery resources
  • Authorizes disaster declarations
  • Oversees major business decisions

Product & Engineering

  • Restore application services
  • Coordinate technical recovery
  • Validate application functionality
  • Prioritize critical system restoration

Customer Success & Support

  • Communicate with customers
  • Track customer impact
  • Manage inbound support requests
  • Coordinate customer updates

Security Team

  • Assess cybersecurity implications
  • Preserve evidence where applicable
  • Coordinate with Incident Response
  • Validate secure restoration

Finance & Administration

  • Support billing continuity
  • Manage vendor communications
  • Track financial impacts
  • Coordinate insurance claims when applicable

6. Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Business continuity planning is based on evaluating the operational impact of service disruptions.

The following factors are considered:

  • Financial impact
  • Customer impact
  • Regulatory obligations
  • Reputational risk
  • Data integrity
  • Security implications
  • Operational dependencies
  • Recovery complexity

The BIA is reviewed periodically and updated as the platform evolves.

Part 2 – Crisis Management, Recovery Operations, and Communications

Version: 1.0

12. Crisis Management Team (CMT)

Linton Business Solutions, LLC ("LBS") maintains a Crisis Management Team responsible for coordinating business continuity activities during significant operational disruptions.

The Crisis Management Team is activated when an event materially impacts Work-A-Bee operations or requires executive coordination.

Primary Responsibilities

  • Assess the severity of the event
  • Declare a business continuity event when appropriate
  • Coordinate recovery activities
  • Prioritize restoration of critical services
  • Allocate personnel and resources
  • Approve customer communications
  • Monitor recovery progress
  • Authorize return to normal operations

13. Crisis Management Roles

The following functional roles participate in continuity management. One individual may fulfill multiple roles depending on company size.

Role Primary Responsibilities
Executive Sponsor Strategic decisions, external communications, disaster declaration
Business Continuity Coordinator Overall coordination of recovery activities
Engineering Lead Application restoration, infrastructure recovery
Security Lead Incident assessment, forensic coordination, security validation
Customer Success Lead Customer communications, support coordination
Operations Lead Vendor coordination, internal logistics
Finance & Administration Financial operations, insurance, vendor payments

Each role should have a designated backup whenever practical.

14. Business Continuity Event Classification

Business disruptions are categorized to ensure an appropriate response.

Level 1 – Minor

Examples:

  • Localized service degradation
  • Isolated application defects
  • Limited customer impact

Typical Response:

  • Operational team manages the issue
  • Executive escalation generally not required

Level 2 – Significant

Examples:

  • Regional cloud disruption
  • Major application failure
  • Authentication outage
  • Large-scale customer impact

Typical Response:

  • Crisis Management Team activated
  • Executive notification
  • Customer communications initiated

Level 3 – Critical

Examples:

  • Complete production outage
  • Widespread cybersecurity incident
  • Data center failure
  • Ransomware event
  • Catastrophic infrastructure failure

Typical Response:

  • Full Business Continuity Plan activation
  • Executive oversight
  • Continuous recovery operations
  • Frequent customer updates

15. Disaster Declaration

A formal Business Continuity Event may be declared when an incident is expected to materially impair critical business operations.

Factors considered include:

  • Estimated duration
  • Number of affected customers
  • Operational impact
  • Security implications
  • Financial impact
  • Regulatory obligations
  • Public reputation

Only authorized executive leadership may formally declare or conclude a disaster event.

16. Incident Coordination

Once activated, the Crisis Management Team will coordinate:

  • Situation assessment
  • Resource assignment
  • Technical recovery
  • Vendor engagement
  • Customer communications
  • Executive reporting
  • Recovery tracking

Operational priorities are reassessed throughout the incident.

17. Internal Communications

Clear communication is essential during business disruptions.

Internal communications may include:

  • Executive briefings
  • Engineering status updates
  • Security advisories
  • Recovery milestones
  • Operational decisions
  • Action assignments

Personnel are expected to report confirmed operational issues promptly through established internal channels.

18. Customer Communications

LBS is committed to providing timely and accurate information during significant service disruptions.

Communications may include:

  • Service interruption notices
  • Maintenance advisories
  • Status updates
  • Estimated restoration timelines (when reasonably available)
  • Recovery progress
  • Incident resolution notices

Communication channels may include:

  • Email
  • Administrative dashboard notifications
  • Customer portal announcements
  • Status page updates
  • Customer Success outreach for enterprise customers

19. Media and Public Communications

Only authorized representatives may issue public statements regarding business continuity events.

Public communications should:

  • Be accurate
  • Avoid speculation
  • Protect confidential information
  • Support customer confidence
  • Comply with legal obligations

Personnel should not communicate publicly regarding incidents unless authorized.

20. Alternate Work Locations

To support continuity of operations, LBS may utilize:

  • Remote work arrangements
  • Alternate office facilities
  • Temporary workspaces
  • Cloud-based collaboration tools

Employees supporting critical operations should have secure remote access whenever practical.

21. Workforce Continuity

LBS seeks to minimize operational disruption caused by personnel shortages.

Continuity measures may include:

  • Cross-training
  • Documented procedures
  • Shared operational knowledge
  • Backup personnel assignments
  • Remote work capabilities
  • Contractor support where appropriate

Critical operational knowledge should not depend on a single individual.

22. Third-Party and Vendor Continuity

Work-A-Bee depends on selected third-party providers.

Business continuity planning includes consideration of:

  • Cloud infrastructure providers
  • Payment processors
  • Authentication providers
  • Email delivery services
  • Domain registration services
  • Monitoring providers
  • Customer communication platforms

LBS monitors key vendor relationships and maintains contingency planning where practical.

23. Remote Operations

Critical business functions should be capable of operating remotely whenever feasible.

Remote operational capabilities may include:

  • Secure administrative access
  • Cloud-based collaboration
  • Ticket management
  • Customer support
  • Engineering activities
  • Monitoring and alerting
  • Incident coordination

Remote access is subject to security controls described in the Security Policy.

24. Manual Workarounds

When automated systems are unavailable, temporary manual procedures may be implemented where feasible.

Examples include:

  • Manual customer communications
  • Manual support ticket tracking
  • Deferred payroll processing
  • Temporary administrative approvals
  • Offline operational documentation

Manual procedures are intended only as temporary continuity measures until normal operations are restored.

25. Resource Management

During continuity events, resources are allocated according to business priorities.

Priority is generally given to:

1. Customer safety and security

2. Protection of Customer Data

3. Restoration of production services

4. Customer communications

5. Regulatory obligations

6. Financial operations

7. Internal administrative functions

Resource allocation may be adjusted as conditions evolve.

26. Operational Recovery Milestones

Recovery activities generally proceed through the following stages:

1. Event identification

2. Initial assessment

3. Business continuity activation

4. Resource mobilization

5. Technical recovery

6. Validation testing

7. Customer communication

8. Service restoration

9. Operational stabilization

10. Post-event review

Completion of one stage may overlap with subsequent stages based on operational needs.

Part 3 – Testing, Recovery Validation, Continuous Improvement, and Governance

Version: 1.0

27. Recovery Validation

Before declaring a recovery complete, Linton Business Solutions, LLC ("LBS") will perform reasonable validation procedures to confirm that critical business functions have been restored.

Validation activities may include:

  • User authentication testing
  • Employee clock-in and clock-out verification
  • Time entry validation
  • Payroll calculation verification
  • Database integrity checks
  • API connectivity testing
  • Administrative portal functionality
  • Reporting verification
  • Security control validation
  • Monitoring and alert confirmation

Recovery is considered complete only after critical services have been verified as operating within acceptable parameters.

28. Return to Normal Operations

Following successful recovery, the Crisis Management Team will coordinate the transition from continuity operations back to standard business operations.

This process includes:

  • Confirming service stability
  • Closing temporary workarounds
  • Reinstating standard operating procedures
  • Returning personnel to normal responsibilities
  • Reviewing temporary security controls
  • Updating operational documentation
  • Communicating recovery completion to stakeholders

Any temporary changes implemented during the event should be documented and either formally adopted or removed.

29. Post-Incident Review

Following significant business continuity events, LBS will conduct a structured post-incident review.

The review should address:

  • Timeline of events
  • Root cause
  • Business impact
  • Customer impact
  • Effectiveness of recovery actions
  • Communication effectiveness
  • Technology performance
  • Vendor performance
  • Opportunities for improvement

Corrective actions will be documented, assigned, and tracked to completion.

30. Corrective Action Management

Recommendations identified during post-incident reviews should be evaluated and prioritized based on risk and business value.

Corrective actions may include:

  • Infrastructure improvements
  • Process updates
  • Security enhancements
  • Documentation revisions
  • Additional training
  • Vendor changes
  • Software improvements
  • Monitoring enhancements

Management will periodically review the status of corrective actions.

31. Business Continuity Testing

The effectiveness of this Business Continuity Plan depends on regular testing.

Testing objectives include:

  • Validating recovery procedures
  • Confirming personnel readiness
  • Identifying documentation gaps
  • Measuring recovery performance
  • Verifying communication processes
  • Improving organizational resilience

Testing results should be documented and reviewed by management.

32. Tabletop Exercises

LBS should conduct periodic tabletop exercises to evaluate decision-making and coordination during simulated disruption scenarios.

Example scenarios include:

  • Cloud provider outage
  • Ransomware attack
  • Database corruption
  • Internet service interruption
  • Identity provider failure
  • Severe weather event
  • Loss of key personnel
  • Third-party vendor failure

Tabletop exercises should identify lessons learned and opportunities to strengthen continuity procedures.

33. Technical Recovery Exercises

Where practical, technical recovery procedures should be periodically validated through controlled testing.

Examples include:

  • Backup restoration
  • Database recovery
  • Infrastructure deployment
  • Disaster recovery failover
  • Identity restoration
  • Configuration recovery
  • Application deployment validation

Testing should avoid unnecessary disruption to production services.

34. Training and Awareness

Personnel with continuity responsibilities should receive training appropriate to their assigned roles.

Training topics may include:

  • Business continuity procedures
  • Incident reporting
  • Communication protocols
  • Recovery responsibilities
  • Remote work procedures
  • Security obligations
  • Disaster declaration process

Training may be supplemented by exercises and periodic awareness reminders.

35. Documentation Management

Business continuity documentation should be:

  • Accurate
  • Current
  • Version controlled
  • Securely stored
  • Accessible to authorized personnel during emergencies

Supporting documentation may include:

  • Recovery procedures
  • Contact lists
  • Vendor information
  • Infrastructure diagrams
  • Critical application inventory
  • Communication templates
  • Checklists

36. Records Retention

Records related to business continuity activities may include:

  • Test results
  • Exercise reports
  • Incident reports
  • Recovery logs
  • Corrective action plans
  • Communication records
  • Disaster declarations
  • Plan revisions

Retention periods should align with legal, regulatory, contractual, and operational requirements.

37. Regulatory and Contractual Considerations

Business continuity activities should support applicable:

  • Contractual commitments
  • Privacy obligations
  • Data protection requirements
  • Industry standards
  • Customer agreements
  • Security policies

Where customer contracts contain additional continuity requirements, those contractual obligations will govern for the applicable customer.

38. Continuous Improvement

Business continuity is an ongoing process.

LBS will periodically evaluate:

  • Emerging risks
  • Operational changes
  • Technology improvements
  • Vendor changes
  • Customer requirements
  • Lessons learned
  • Industry best practices

Updates to this plan should reflect significant organizational or technical changes.

39. Plan Review

This Business Continuity Plan should be reviewed:

  • At least annually
  • Following significant incidents
  • After major infrastructure changes
  • Following organizational restructuring
  • After major product releases
  • When significant risks are identified

Reviews should verify that the plan remains accurate and operationally effective.

Appendix B – Business Continuity Recovery Checklist

The following checklist provides a high-level sequence for continuity operations:

  1. Identify and verify the disruption.
  2. Assess business impact.
  3. Determine event severity.
  4. Activate the Crisis Management Team if required.
  5. Protect personnel and critical assets.
  6. Preserve Customer Data and evidence, where applicable.
  7. Notify key internal stakeholders.
  8. Communicate with affected customers as appropriate.
  9. Restore Tier 1 services.
  10. Restore Tier 2 services.
  11. Validate system functionality.
  12. Resume normal operations.
  13. Conduct a post-incident review.
  14. Document lessons learned and corrective actions.

  • Infrastructure-as-Code repositories

The inventory should identify system owners, dependencies, recovery priorities, and recovery objectives.
